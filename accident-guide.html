What to do after a car accident in Sheridan

A wreck is stressful and disorienting. Keep this checklist in mind — or in your glovebox — so you can protect yourself, your passengers, and your claim.

At the scene

  1. Get to safety. If the vehicles are drivable and it's safe, move them out of traffic and turn on your hazard lights. On I-90 or a busy Sheridan street, staying in a damaged car is the real danger.
  2. Check for injuries and call 911. Wyoming law requires reporting any crash involving injury, death, or significant property damage. When in doubt, call — a police report is invaluable for your claim.
  3. Document everything. Photograph all vehicles, license plates, the overall scene, road conditions, and any visible damage from multiple angles. Photos win disputes.
  4. Exchange information. Names, phone numbers, insurance carriers and policy numbers, driver's license numbers, and vehicle details for everyone involved.
  5. Find witnesses. If anyone saw what happened, ask for their name and number before they leave.

Don't admit fault at the scene

Stay factual with the other driver and the police. Fault is determined later by the insurers and the evidence — an apology in the moment can be used against you.

Dealing with insurance

Report the claim to your insurer promptly. Be honest and factual, and write down your claim number. You'll be assigned an adjuster who will estimate the damage — but their first estimate is not the final word.

You choose your repair shop — not the insurance company

This is the single most important thing drivers in Wyoming don't realize: your insurer cannot legally require you to use a specific body shop. They may "recommend" a Direct Repair Program (DRP) shop, but the choice is always yours. A shop that works for you — not the insurer's bottom line — will advocate for the complete, correct repair your vehicle needs.

"Preferred" shops aren't always in your interest

DRP shops agree to insurer pricing and procedures to get steered work. That can mean used or aftermarket parts and shortcuts on labor. An independent, certified shop documents the damage thoroughly and fights for OEM parts and factory repair procedures.

Choosing a collision repair shop

  • Experience & training. Look for an established shop with experienced technicians and ongoing training in current repair techniques.
  • Warranty. A reputable shop stands behind its work in writing. Ask about a lifetime workmanship warranty.
  • Right tools for the job. Make sure the shop has up-to-date equipment and uses quality, properly color-matched paint for a lasting repair.
  • Transparency. You should get a clear written estimate and updates throughout the repair.
